# Parent Handbook

## Welcome
Welcome to BrightPath English Learning Center. This handbook helps parents understand how to support children and teenagers in English learning without creating unnecessary pressure. It is designed for families whose children attend our center and use a parent portal or AI Parent Assistant to track progress.

Our curriculum is structured around the **Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR): A1, A2, B1, B2, C1, and C2**. Parents do not need to be English experts to help their children succeed in this pathway. The most useful support is steady attendance, calm interest, healthy routines, honest communication, and encouragement after mistakes. This handbook explains how our CEFR pathway works, how to read learning information, how to respond to common challenges, and how to work with teachers in a productive way.

## Understanding Our CEFR Curriculum Pathway

### What Parents Should Understand
BrightPath does not use a "one-size-fits-all" approach based strictly on age. Course placement is based on CEFR evidence, age, learning goals, maturity, attendance capacity, writing samples, speaking performance, and teacher recommendation.

Here is a quick reference to our pathway and rough IELTS equivalents:

| CEFR Level | Common Description | Rough IELTS Reference |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **A1** | Beginner / Basic user | 1.0-2.5 |
| **A2** | Elementary / Basic user | 3.0-3.5 |
| **B1** | Intermediate / Independent user | 4.0-5.0 |
| **B2** | Upper-intermediate / Independent user | 5.5-6.5 |
| **C1** | Advanced / Proficient user | 7.0-8.0 |
| **C2** | Mastery / Proficient user | 8.5-9.0 |

*Note: Score and IELTS references are placement guides, not automatic certificate guarantees. Final placement considers real performance across all four skills.*

### Choosing the Right Pathway
Parents should choose a course based on the student's current CEFR skill profile. For example, a student who wants IELTS but lacks paragraph control may need B1 or B2 writing support before full IELTS preparation. The best pathway is usually the one that creates steady progress, protects confidence, and gives teachers accurate evidence.

## Understanding Your Child's English Learning Journey

### What Parents Should Understand
English progress across the CEFR levels is rarely a straight line. A student may improve quickly in A2 vocabulary but need more time to speak confidently, or they may understand B2 reading texts but struggle to organize ideas in writing. Parents should look for patterns over several weeks instead of reacting to one lesson, one quiz, or one difficult assignment. Furthermore, English development heavily depends on attendance, previous exposure, home routines, school workload, and willingness to revise after feedback.

### Practical Guidance
- Look at attendance, homework completion, skill scores, and teacher comments together.
- Ask whether the student can use language independently in real-life situations, not only whether they can remember answers.
- Notice confidence, willingness to try, and ability to revise after feedback.
- Celebrate small improvements such as clearer pronunciation, better sentence control, or more consistent homework habits.
- Use progress reports to plan support, not to pressure the child.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Set a short review time after each class for the student to explain what they learned.
- Keep a vocabulary notebook with example sentences instead of only translations.
- Ask the student to teach one new expression to a parent each week.
- Review teacher feedback before the next class so the student returns prepared.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Many parents focus only on test scores. Scores matter, but they do not show the full picture. A child who scores 72 but revises carefully, attends regularly, and asks questions may be building stronger long-term habits for the B2 or C1 levels than a child who scores 90 while avoiding difficult tasks.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** I saw your writing score was lower this week. What part was difficult?
**Student:** I had ideas but did not know how to connect them.
**Parent:** Let's read the teacher's comment and choose one thing to improve before the next writing task.

## How to Read Progress Reports

### What Parents Should Understand
A progress report is a learning snapshot. It usually combines scores, teacher observations, attendance, homework, and recommended next steps to prepare for the next CEFR level. The most useful part is often not the number but the explanation of what the student can do now and what they should practice next.

### Practical Guidance
- Read the summary first, then check individual skills.
- Look for repeated comments across reports, such as recurring grammar errors or stronger speaking participation.
- Separate ability from behavior. Weak writing organization is different from missing homework.
- Ask the teacher for examples if a comment feels too general.
- Compare the student's current performance with their own previous performance rather than with classmates.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- After each report, choose one home goal for the next two weeks.
- Keep the goal specific, such as using past tense correctly in a diary paragraph (for A2/B1 learners) or improving thesis control (for C1 learners).
- Review the goal with the student at the end of the week.
- Bring questions to the next parent meeting instead of waiting until the final report.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Do not turn every report into a lecture. Children often stop sharing honestly when reports always lead to criticism. A calm review teaches them that feedback is normal and useful.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** Your report says you participate more in pair work than whole-class speaking. That is good information. Would it help to practice one short answer before class so you feel ready to speak up?

## Supporting Listening Skills at Home

### What Parents Should Understand
Listening improves through regular exposure and active attention. Depending on their level (A1 to C2), students need to hear English at the right complexity, notice key words, predict meaning, and listen again for details. Passive background audio is less effective than short, focused listening tasks.

### Practical Guidance
- Use audio that is slightly challenging but not overwhelming.
- Ask the student to listen for purpose, such as main idea, speaker opinion, or three key details.
- Replay short sections and ask what changed after the second listening.
- Use subtitles carefully. First listen without subtitles, then check with subtitles if needed.
- Encourage the student to notice pronunciation features such as word stress and connected speech.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Listen to a three-minute English clip twice a week.
- Ask the student to summarize the clip in two sentences.
- Write down five useful phrases from the audio.
- Have the student imitate one short sentence with natural rhythm.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Playing long videos while the student is tired rarely builds listening skill. Short, repeated, purposeful listening is more useful than long passive exposure.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** What was the speaker mainly talking about?
**Student:** A school project.
**Parent:** Good. What was one problem they mentioned? Let's listen one more time and check.

## Supporting Speaking Confidence

### What Parents Should Understand
Speaking confidence grows when students have repeated chances to speak without fear of embarrassment. Whether practicing A1 introductions or C1 debates, parents can help by creating low-pressure English moments at home and by valuing clear communication over perfect grammar.

### Practical Guidance
- Ask open but simple questions that the student can answer with familiar language.
- Let the student finish before correcting errors.
- Correct one important pattern at a time instead of interrupting every sentence.
- Encourage recording short speaking practice so the student can hear improvement over time.
- Praise effort, volume, eye contact, and willingness to try.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Have a two-minute English check-in twice a week.
- Use familiar topics such as food, school, hobbies, weekend plans, or books.
- Ask the student to prepare one opinion sentence and one reason.
- Record a monthly speaking sample and compare it with an older one.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Do not demand advanced speaking before the student has language to use. Pressure to speak perfectly can make students avoid speaking altogether.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** Tell me one thing you liked today in English.
**Student:** I liked art class because we painted.
**Parent:** Nice. Can you add one more detail about the painting?

## Supporting Reading Development

### What Parents Should Understand
Reading development scales with the CEFR levels—from short, controlled sentences at A1 to complex academic texts at C1/C2. Students need texts that match their level and enough time to read without translating every word.

### Practical Guidance
- Encourage the student to guess meaning from context before using a dictionary.
- Ask questions about main idea, character motivation, evidence, and opinion.
- Use graded readers for younger or lower-level students (A1-A2).
- For advanced exam students (B2-C1), practice skimming, scanning, and identifying distractors.
- Make reading visible by keeping a reading log with title, date, difficulty, and one response sentence.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Read for ten to fifteen minutes three times a week.
- Choose one paragraph and ask the student to underline key words.
- Ask the student to retell the text in their own words.
- Review new vocabulary after two days and again after one week.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Translating every sentence into the home language can make reading slow and frustrating. Translation can help sometimes, but students should also practice understanding English directly.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** Which sentence shows why the character was worried?
**Student:** This one.
**Parent:** Good. What word gives you that clue?

## Supporting Writing Without Doing the Work

### What Parents Should Understand
Writing improves through planning, drafting, feedback, and revision. From B1 paragraph building to C2 academic research synthesis, parents can support the process without writing for the student. The goal is to help the child think more clearly and revise more independently.

### Practical Guidance
- Ask the student to explain the task in their own words before writing.
- Help the student brainstorm ideas, but let them choose and write the sentences.
- Use teacher rubrics to check organization, grammar focus, vocabulary, and task response.
- Encourage revision after feedback rather than treating the first draft as final.
- Keep examples of improved drafts so the student can see progress.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Before writing, ask for a simple outline with beginning, middle, and end.
- After writing, ask the student to check one target, such as verb tense or paragraph topic sentence.
- Use a timer for focused drafting.
- Review teacher comments together and rewrite only the sentences that need practice.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Parents sometimes rewrite a child's essay to make it look better. This hides the student's real needs and prevents the teacher from giving accurate support.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** I will not rewrite this paragraph, but I can ask questions. What is your main idea? Which sentence gives an example? Where could you add a reason?

## Building Vocabulary That Students Actually Use

### What Parents Should Understand
Vocabulary knowledge is more than memorizing translations. Students need to know meaning, pronunciation, spelling, word family, common collocations, and how to use the word in a sentence appropriate to their CEFR band.

### Practical Guidance
- Group words by topic or function rather than memorizing random lists.
- Practice words in sentences that relate to the student's life.
- Review old words regularly because vocabulary is forgotten without spaced practice.
- Notice useful phrases, not only single words.
- For exam preparation, learn academic word families and common question language.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Choose eight to ten target words each week.
- Write one personal example sentence for each word.
- Sort words into easy, medium, and difficult piles.
- Use three new words in a short spoken summary.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
A student may recognize many words but still not use them accurately. Productive vocabulary takes more time than recognition vocabulary.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** You wrote 'improve' in your list. Can you say one sentence about how to improve speaking?
**Student:** I can improve speaking by practicing every week.

## Helping Students Prepare for Assessments

### What Parents Should Understand
Assessment preparation should review skills and reduce anxiety. Whether it is an A2 module test or a B2 IELTS Foundation mock exam, it should not become memorization of answers or last-minute pressure. Students perform better when they understand the format, know the target skills, and practice under realistic conditions.

### Practical Guidance
- Review the assessment scope and CEFR skill focus.
- Practice similar task types without copying actual test items.
- Use timed practice for older students when timing is part of the assessment.
- Review mistakes after practice and identify one or two patterns to improve.
- Help the student sleep well before the assessment.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Start review several days before the test.
- Use short daily practice instead of one long cram session.
- Ask the student to explain the instructions for each task type.
- Prepare materials the night before class.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Do not ask the AI assistant or another person for answers to active assessments. This damages trust and prevents the teacher from understanding the student's real ability.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** The test includes reading for main idea and vocabulary in context. Let's practice one similar text, then talk about the mistakes.

## When to Contact the Teacher

### What Parents Should Understand
Parents should contact the teacher when they need learning interpretation, classroom observations, or guidance on how to support the student's progression to the next level. Teachers can help most when questions are specific and connected to actual learning evidence.

### Practical Guidance
- Contact the teacher if a skill score drops suddenly across more than one task.
- Ask for guidance when homework regularly takes too long or causes distress.
- Request clarification if teacher comments or CEFR expectations are unclear.
- Share important home context such as school exam pressure, health issues, or schedule overload.
- Use admin channels for billing, schedule, and enrollment topics.

### Home Routine Suggestions
- Prepare two or three specific questions before a meeting.
- Bring examples such as homework pages, report comments, or dashboard trends.
- Ask for next steps that can be practiced at home.
- Follow up after two weeks to check whether the support plan is working.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Sending many urgent messages for routine issues can slow communication. Use the dashboard and AI assistant for simple explanations, then contact the teacher for professional judgment.

### Example Parent Conversation
**Parent:** I noticed three writing comments mention sentence connection. Could you suggest one home activity for practicing linking words before they move to the next course?

## Using the Parent Dashboard and AI Assistant

The dashboard gives parents a clearer view of attendance, homework, skill scores, teacher feedback, assessment analysis, and recommended actions. The AI assistant helps explain this information in parent-friendly language.

### Practical Guidance

- Use AI recommendations as practical suggestions, then confirm serious concerns with a teacher.

- Save questions for the teacher when the assistant says human review is needed.

### Common Mistake to Avoid
Do not treat the AI assistant as a replacement for the teacher. It can summarize and explain, but it does not observe the student in class and cannot approve policy exceptions.

## A Simple Monthly Parent Checklist
- Check attendance and homework completion.
- Read teacher feedback and identify one repeated strength.
- Identify one skill to monitor, such as writing organization, reading inference, speaking fluency, listening detail, grammar accuracy, or vocabulary use.
- Ask the student what felt easier this month and what still feels difficult.
- Choose one home routine for the next month.
- Contact the teacher if the issue requires professional interpretation or if progress has stalled despite consistent effort.

## Final Guidance for Parents
Children learn best when adults are consistent, patient, and specific. A useful parent response sounds like this: *"I can see what you are practicing, I know what the teacher wants you to improve, and I will help you build the habit."* The center, the teacher, the parent dashboard, and the AI Parent Assistant all support the same goal: helping the student become a more confident, independent English learner at every stage of their CEFR journey.
